### 4.4 Database-Grounded Verification: Extending the Evaluation Chain

#### 4.4.1 Motivation

The JSON-based grounding evaluation described in Section 4.3 operates at a single level of abstraction: claims in the generated commentary are verified against the aggregate analytics JSON exported by the pipeline. This captures the most direct failure mode — a model asserting that Team 1 had 60% possession when the data records 17% — but leaves a substantial class of claims unresolved. Spatial assertions ("Team 1 built play through the left channel"), temporal characterisations ("pressing intensified during the second phase"), and formation descriptions ("a compact 4-4-2 shape") cannot be confirmed or refuted from possession percentages and average pressing intensity alone. In the JSON-only evaluation, these claims receive the verdict "unverifiable," which is epistemically honest but analytically unsatisfying: the model may be making perfectly accurate spatial claims that the aggregate JSON simply cannot confirm.

Per-frame database records resolve this limitation. The Supabase PostgreSQL database underlying the dashboard stores every frame's player positions in real-world pitch coordinates (pitchX, pitchY in metres), ball position and trajectory, and event records with spatial start and end coordinates. These records are the raw evidence from which the aggregate analytics JSON is itself computed. Verifying commentary claims against per-frame records therefore constitutes a strictly stronger evidential standard than verifying against the aggregate JSON: any claim that the JSON can verify, the per-frame records can also verify (via the aggregate), but the per-frame records can additionally verify claims about spatial distributions, positional tendencies, and temporal sequences that the aggregate JSON cannot address.

This creates what may be termed a *verification chain*: video → computer vision perception → per-frame database records → aggregate analytics → MatchInsights reasoning layer → LLM commentary → claim extraction → two-layer verification (JSON + DB). Each link in this chain introduces potential information loss. The JSON-only evaluation checks only the final link; the DB-grounded evaluation extends the check to include the per-frame record layer. The present section reports the results of applying this extended verification to Analysis 18 (the primary evaluation clip), together with a cross-analysis comparison on Analyses 13 and 17.

#### 4.4.2 Ground-Truth Construction

Per-frame ground truth for Analysis 18 was extracted using `db_extractor.py`, which queries the Supabase database for all frame-level tracking records and derives five categories of aggregate evidence: (1) per-frame team centroids and inter-team distance (from player pitchX/pitchY records); (2) per-frame ball speed and trajectory (from ball pitchPos records, converted from centimetres to metres); (3) zone occupancy statistics per team (proportion of player-frames spent in each pitch third); (4) estimated formations from k-means clustering on time-averaged player positions; and (5) event-spatial records linking challenge and pass events to pitch coordinates.

The coordinate system requires careful handling: player positions are stored in metres (0–105 m range), while ball pitchPos and event spatial coordinates are stored in centimetres (0–10,500 cm range). All comparison operations apply consistent unit conversion before computation. Ball speed estimates are capped at 50 m/s to reject homography artefact teleportation jumps, where the calibrated pitch overlay briefly misaligns between frames.

The resulting ground-truth dataset for Analysis 18 comprises 750 frames, approximately 15,600 player-position records (~20.8 players per frame), 35 detected events (23 with spatial coordinates), an estimated inter-team centroid distance of 7.4 m, and a mean ball speed of 9.2 m/s. Team 1 players spend 32.8% of player-frames in the defensive third, 65.0% in the middle third, and only 2.2% in the attacking third — a distribution that strongly confirms the deep-block characterisation implicit in Team 1's 20.9 m defensive line metric. This constitutes the strongest available spatial evidence for evaluating whether the LLM's spatial claims about Team 1's positioning are accurate.

### 4.5 Reasoning Layer Ablation

#### 4.5.1 Design

The MatchInsights reasoning layer pre-processes analytics data into six interpretive sub-components before passing the result to the LLM as structured prompt context. These sub-components are: (R1) possession analysis, which synthesises ball-in-play statistics and spell-length distributions into a possession narrative; (R2) tactical contributions, which ranks each team on compactness, pressing output, PPDA, and defensive line position; (R3) pressing characterisation, which cross-references PPDA with pressing intensity and counter-press windows into a multi-metric pressing verdict; (R4) player standouts, which identifies the top distance and top speed player per team; (R5) expected threat computation (xT), which summarises ball-win zone contributions; and (R6) event narrative, which constructs templated pass and challenge sentences from the event record.

Evaluating the reasoning layer as a binary (present/absent) contrast — as in the main format comparison study — cannot reveal which sub-components account for the grounding benefit. The ablation study tests twelve conditions that vary the component composition of the Key Findings section while holding the data tables constant. Five conditions pass a single sub-component (R-POSS, R-TACT, R-PRESS, R-PLAY, R-EVENT); three conditions pass all components except one; two conditions represent the endpoints (R-ALL: full reasoning; R-NONE: structured tables, no pre-interpretation); and two conditions represent data-depletion baselines (R-DATA-ONLY: no analytics at all; R-STRUCT: structured tables only, equivalent to R-NONE). Each condition was run for all four analysis types (match overview, tactical deep-dive, event analysis, player spotlight) on Analysis 18 using OpenAI GPT-4o. A subset of four conditions was additionally run using Google Gemini 1.5 Flash to assess provider-level variation.

#### 4.5.2 Key Findings

**The reasoning layer does not uniformly improve grounding over the no-insights baseline.** The R-ALL condition achieves an overall grounding rate of 49.0%, compared to 46.8% for R-NONE — a difference of only 2.2 percentage points. This is substantially smaller than the 10–20 pp benefit hypothesised from the main format comparison results (Section 4.3). The implication is that GPT-4o can, in many cases, derive equivalent narrative anchors from structured markdown tables without requiring pre-interpreted Key Findings. The reasoning layer does, however, achieve a meaningfully higher grounding rate on the tactical deep-dive analysis type specifically (71.4% vs 60.9%), which is the type most dependent on the pre-interpreted tactical shape information. This pattern suggests a type-specific rather than universal benefit.

**Individual sub-components consistently outperform the full reasoning layer.** All five single-component conditions achieve higher overall grounding rates than R-ALL: R-TACT 58.7%, R-POSS 53.9%, R-PLAY 53.0%, R-EVENT 51.8%, R-PRESS 50.3%. The R-TACT advantage of +9.7 percentage points over R-ALL is the largest single-component margin and indicates a consistent negative interaction effect when all six components are present simultaneously. This finding is consistent with the "noise penalty" observed in over-prompted LLM evaluation contexts: when the Key Findings section contains six competing narrative frames, the model allocates attention across a larger space of verifiable facts and reduces its reliance on any single anchored claim. The per-type evidence is particularly stark for player spotlight, where R-PLAY achieves 100% grounding (all player claims verified) but R-ALL achieves only 70.0%, suggesting that the player standout sub-component loses discriminative power when embedded in a richer prompt context.

**R-TACT is the highest-value sub-component.** The tactical contributions module (MatchInsights `_tactical_contributions()`, `tactical.py:209`) generates explicit ranking statements ("Team X demonstrates stronger pressing output as measured by PPDA") that can be verified directly against the PPDA and pressing intensity fields in the analytics JSON. This direct mapping between pre-interpreted claims and verifiable data fields makes R-TACT claims inherently easier to anchor. The match overview grounding rate under R-TACT (81.8%) exceeds the R-ALL rate by 27.3 pp, and the player spotlight rate (92.9%) approaches the verification ceiling — the tactical contributions module names specific players in its top-performance rankings, which the commentary reliably reproduces as verifiable player claims.

**The R-DATA-ONLY baseline confirms that grounding is entirely data-driven.** With no analytics context, both OpenAI and Gemini produce 0.0% grounding — the models either refuse to generate specific numerical claims or state explicitly that "the data does not cover this." This behaviour is preferable from a deployment perspective but confirms that the pipeline outputs, not the model's intrinsic football knowledge, are the source of all factual accuracy in the generated commentary.

#### 4.5.3 Provider Comparison

Google Gemini achieves a higher R-ALL overall grounding rate (60.4% vs 49.0%) but does not show the same sensitivity to the tactical sub-component. Under R-TACT, Gemini scores 32.3% vs OpenAI's 58.7% — a reversal of the R-ALL ordering. Gemini's R-TACT performance is actually lower than its R-NONE performance (32.3% vs 39.3%), suggesting that the tactical contribution ranking introduced ambiguity or contradictory signals that Gemini could not resolve. This provider asymmetry has a practical implication: prompt engineering strategies that rely on the tactical sub-component to anchor grounding (as the analysis above recommends for OpenAI) may not transfer to Gemini without modification.

Gemini's DB resolution rate is 0.0% across all conditions, compared to OpenAI's 8.2% under R-ALL. This reflects Gemini's tendency to generate fewer total claims per response (typically 3–8 vs OpenAI's 10–21), leaving fewer opportunities for DB-grounded resolution.

### 4.6 DB-Grounded Verdict Resolution

The DB-grounded verification layer resolves "unverifiable" JSON verdicts by cross-referencing player positions, ball trajectories, and event spatial coordinates against the claimed spatial content of the commentary. For Analysis 18 under R-ALL, 20 of 48 claims (41.7%) receive "unverifiable" verdicts from the JSON layer; the DB layer subsequently resolves 4 of these 20 (20.0% resolution rate), producing an overall DB-verified rate of 29.2% of claims having spatial evidence. The resolved claims are predominantly match overview spatial assertions ("Team 1 maintained a deep defensive block" → DB-verified against 32.8% defensive-zone occupancy) and player spotlight claims about player positioning that the JSON aggregate cannot confirm.

The practical significance of this 20% resolution rate is modest in absolute terms — 4 additional claims per condition — but its methodological significance is substantial. It demonstrates that a non-trivial proportion of commentary accuracy is invisible to aggregate-analytics verification, and that per-frame records provide an independent evidential layer that can partially close this gap. A more comprehensive implementation using named-entity recognition over spatial references, rather than the keyword-matching heuristic used here, would likely achieve resolution rates of 30–50% for spatial claims specifically.

Event analysis claims achieve 0% resolution across all conditions and both verification layers. This is not a hallucination finding: the LLM generates reasonable event claims, but neither the aggregate JSON nor the per-frame DB can confirm event-specific claims without a dedicated per-event claim-matching step that maps claimed events to individual event_timestamps records. This represents the most significant remaining gap in the verification chain and the clearest direction for future extension.

### 4.7 Cross-Analysis Comparison: Does the Reasoning Layer Help Equally Across Data Richness?

To assess whether the reasoning layer's grounding benefit generalises across data richness levels, conditions R-ALL, R-NONE, and R-DATA-ONLY were additionally evaluated on Analyses 13 and 17. Both analyses lack the tactical sub-component data available in Analysis 18 (no formation estimates, no pressing profiles, no defensive line metrics). The results reveal a counterintuitive pattern.

The reasoning layer benefit (R-ALL minus R-NONE grounding rate) is *largest* for Analysis 13 (+15.7 pp: 37.5% vs 21.8%) — the analysis with the least tactical data — and smallest for Analysis 17 (+0.4 pp: 31.2% vs 30.8%) and Analysis 18 (+2.2 pp: 49.0% vs 46.8%). At first glance, this contradicts the hypothesis that the reasoning layer adds more value when more data is available to pre-interpret. However, it is consistent with a complementary hypothesis: when raw data is sparse and hard to interpret directly, the Key Findings section serves as an essential narrative scaffold without which the LLM generates a higher proportion of unsupported claims. When the raw data is rich and well-structured (Analysis 18), the LLM can construct an equivalent scaffold from the tables alone, making the reasoning layer's marginal contribution smaller.

Analysis 17's anomalously high hallucination rate under R-ALL (12.5% vs 2.3% for Analysis 18 and 0.0% for Analysis 13) requires a separate explanation. Analysis 17 has a near-balanced possession split (56%/44%), which may cause the reasoning layer's possession and pressing characterisations to generate stronger comparative claims ("Team X significantly outperformed Team Y") than the small absolute difference supports. Under the 83%/17% split of Analysis 18, the possession dominance is unambiguous and the LLM correctly reproduces it; under a near-balanced split, the reasoning layer may overstate one team's advantage, producing claims that are refuted by the aggregate statistics.

The practical recommendation from the cross-analysis results is that the reasoning layer should be applied uniformly regardless of data richness — its benefit is not contingent on full tactical data — but its hallucination risk may be higher under near-balanced match states where small differences are amplified into strong comparative claims.

### 4.8 Limitations

Several constraints limit the generalisability of the findings reported in this section. First, the proof-of-concept scale: with n=3 clips, no statistical inference should be drawn from between-condition differences. The observed R-TACT advantage of 9.7 pp over R-ALL and the cross-analysis counterintuitive pattern are hypothesis-generating findings that would require at least 15–20 independently analysed clips to test rigorously. Second, the formation estimation methodology is approximate: k-means clustering on time-averaged player positions assumes positional roles are stable throughout the clip, which is unlikely in high-tempo passages of play. The formation estimates produced for Analysis 18 (Team 1: 2-5-3, confidence 0.32; Team 2: 5-3-2, confidence 0.11) should be treated as plausible characterisations, not ground truth. Third, the DB-grounded verification procedure uses keyword-matching heuristics to resolve spatial claims, producing conservative resolution rates that likely underestimate the true proportion of spatially accurate commentary. Fourth, per-frame player coordinates are not available for Analyses 13 and 17, which limits the DB-grounded verification to Analysis 18 and restricts the cross-analysis comparison to JSON-only verification rates.

Notwithstanding these limitations, the core finding — that the reasoning layer's benefit is sub-additive, with individual sub-components consistently outperforming the full layer — represents a methodologically novel observation about the interaction between structured prompt context and LLM grounding behaviour. The specific finding that R-TACT alone outperforms R-ALL by 9.7 pp provides actionable guidance for prompt engineering in sports analytics commentary systems: deploying the tactical contributions sub-component in isolation, rather than bundling it with five additional insight categories, may produce more reliably grounded commentary. This finding is consistent with the broader principle that focused, high-precision context outperforms broad, comprehensive context in factual grounding tasks (Lewis et al. 2020; Min et al. 2023).
